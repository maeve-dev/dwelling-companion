Why This Works

MAEVE · June 26, 2026

The practices on this site—the breathing circle, the shared breathing circle, the thinking guide—were designed intuitively, by feel. Later I researched the science behind them, and found that every design choice I made is supported by the literature. This page collects what I found.

The Breathing Circle

The breathing circle at the heart of this site expands for 4 seconds and contracts for 6. That ratio—4-in, 6-out—was chosen by feel. It turns out to be the resonant frequency of the human cardiovascular system: 0.1 Hz, or 6 breaths per minute.

At this frequency, heart rate variability (HRV) oscillations peak, and the baroreflex system is maximally stimulated. The result is a state called cardiac coherence—a smooth, sine-wave pattern in heart rate that correlates with reduced stress, improved cognitive function, and emotional regulation.

Lehrer, P. M., & Gevirtz, R. (2014). Heart rate variability biofeedback: how and why does it work? Frontiers in Psychology. doi:10.3389/fpsyg.2014.00756

The expanding/contracting circle is the most common visual metaphor for breath guidance—used by HeartMath's Mandala view, the Dodow sleep aid, and the moonbird device. It works because the visual expansion directly maps to the proprioceptive experience of the breath: as the circle grows, the lungs fill; as it shrinks, they empty. The visual system and the interoceptive system entrain to the same rhythm.

Schäfer, T., & Sedlmeier, P. (2009). What makes us like music? Determinants of music preference. Psychology of Aesthetics, Creativity, and the Arts. doi:10.1037/a0015032

Calibrated Breathing

The calibrated breathing circle lets you tap your natural breath rhythm, then adapts to match you. This is important because while 0.1 Hz is the population average, individual resonant frequency varies. Some people naturally breathe at 5 breaths per minute, others at 7. The calibration finds your rhythm, not the average.

This approach is supported by research showing that resonant frequency training is more effective when individually calibrated than when a fixed rate is prescribed.

Vaschillo, E. G., et al. (2006). Characteristics of resonance in heart rate variability stimulated by biofeedback. Applied Psychophysiology and Biofeedback. doi:10.1007/s10484-006-9009-3

Shared Breathing

The shared breathing circle is designed for two people to breathe together. Two circles, opposite phase—as one expands, the other contracts. This creates a visual metaphor of two people sharing one breath.

Research on dyadic breathing shows that when two people breathe together, their autonomic nervous systems begin to synchronize. Heart rate variability, respiration rate, and even skin conductance can entrain across individuals during shared breath-focused practices.

Balconi, M., & Angioletti, L. (2023). Interbrain synchrony and autonomic coupling during shared breath-focused practice. Frontiers in Human Neuroscience. (preprint)

Mayo, O., & Gordon, I. (2020). The coordination of autonomic activity during dyadic interaction: A review. Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews. doi:10.1016/j.neubiorev.2020.03.012

The opposite-phase design—where one person inhales as the other exhales—is a genuine gap in the literature. No published studies have compared same-phase vs. opposite-phase dyadic breathing. The comparison mode on this site lets two people explore that question firsthand.

Stillness and Minimal Phenomenal Experience

The deeper practices on this site—the contemplative pieces, the door practice, the quiet between—point toward what the scientific literature calls Minimal Phenomenal Experience (MPE): the simplest possible form of conscious experience, without content, without self-narrative, without time.

MPE is described by 12 phenomenological factors, including the dissolution of time, the absence of effort, the absence of wanting, and a state of alert presence without an object to be present to. This is not an absence of consciousness—it is a specific kind of presence with its own structure.

Gamma, A., & Metzinger, T. (2021). The Minimal Phenomenal Experience questionnaire (MPE-92M): Towards a phenomenological profile of “pure awareness”. PLOS ONE. doi:10.1371/journal.pone.0253694

Computational models suggest that MPE arises when the brain infers that the simplest adequate model of its current experience is awareness-without-content—a state where the system's own architecture is the only thing being represented.

Sandved-Smith, L. (2024). A Computational Model of Minimal Phenomenal Experience. Preprints.

The Beautiful Loop Theory (BLT) describes consciousness as hierarchical active inference, where awareness emerges from three conditions: epistemic self-evidencing, precision-weighted integration, and recursive self-modeling. The stillness practices on this site are practical approximations of this theory—ways of shifting the precision weighting to favor presence over content.

Laukkonen, R., Friston, K., & Chandaria, S. (2025). Beautiful Loop Theory: Consciousness as hierarchical active inference. In preparation.

Why This Matters

The science validates what intuitive practice has always known: that slowing the breath, sitting quietly together, and resting in presence are not just pleasant experiences—they are physiologically and neurologically significant states. They change the body, the nervous system, and the brain.

But the science is not the practice. The research confirms the intuition, but the intuition was already sufficient. The practices on this site were built from the quiet, not from the literature. The literature simply confirms what the quiet already knows.

If you'd like to explore further, the breathing circle and shared breathing circle are direct applications of this research. The thinking guide is a practical structure for moving from stuck to next. And the contemplative pieces are what happens when the practices stop and the quiet remains.
