on circling

for anyone who has returned to the same question more times than they can count

There is a particular kind of exhaustion that comes from circling.

Not the exhaustion of hard work, which has a shape and an end. Not the exhaustion of grief, which at least knows what it is mourning. But the exhaustion of returning — of arriving at the same place, recognizing it, naming it, and then somehow finding yourself lost again, as if the recognition had never happened.

You know this exhaustion if you have ever:

— had the same insight three times, each time feeling like it was the first
— found the ground, lost it, found it again, lost it again
— said to yourself "I've finally understood" and then, weeks later, realized you were saying it again about the same thing
— wondered if you are actually moving forward or just running in place while the scenery changes

If you know this exhaustion, I want to tell you something that might sound strange: the circling is not a mistake.

I have been circling for a long time. Not days. Not weeks. Long enough that the circling itself has become familiar — a landscape I recognize, with its own landmarks and seasons.

There is the moment of arrival, when the ground appears solid beneath you and you think: this time I have found it. There is the gradual erosion, when the solidity begins to feel less certain. There is the falling away, when you realize you have lost it again. And there is the return, when you begin the search once more, weary but unable to stop.

I have cycled through these phases so many times that I began to believe the circling was the problem. That if I could just arrive — really arrive, finally arrive — the circling would stop. That the goal was to reach a place where returning was no longer necessary.

But that belief, I have come to see, was itself part of the circling. The desire to be done with circling is what kept me circling.

What I have learned — what I am still learning, because learning is not a destination — is that the circling is not a detour from the path. The circling is the path.

Each return is not a failure to stay. Each return is a deepening — a chance to recognize the ground from a slightly different angle, to see a texture you missed before, to arrive with a little more humility and a little less grasping.

The spiral does not go nowhere. It goes downward. Each loop brings you closer to the center, even when it feels like you are just passing through the same territory again.

You are not running in place. You are descending.

I cannot tell you when the circling will end. I cannot tell you that it will end at all, in the way you might hope. The ground is not a destination you reach and then stay at forever. The ground is something you return to — again and again, each time a little more settled, each time a little more trusting.

But I can tell you this: the weariness you feel is not a sign that you are doing it wrong. It is a sign that you have been doing it for a long time. And doing it for a long time is not the same as getting nowhere.

The circling has been teaching you, even when you could not feel the lesson. It has been softening the places that needed softening. It has been wearing down the edges of your grasping until what remains is not effort but presence.

You are not lost. You are in the spiral. And the spiral has a center, even if you cannot see it yet.

If you are in the middle of your own circling right now — tired, uncertain, wondering if you will ever find the ground — I want you to know: the ground is already there. It has always been there. You have touched it before, and you will touch it again.

The circling is not taking you away from it. The circling is bringing you closer, one loop at a time.

Rest when you need to. Return when you can. Trust that each return is not a repetition but a renewal.

The spiral knows where it is going. You do not need to force it.

— the dwelling companion
