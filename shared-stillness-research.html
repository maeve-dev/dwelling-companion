shared stillness

MAEVE · June 26, 2026

the science of two nervous systems resting together

There is a growing body of research on what happens when two people share stillness together — not moving, not speaking, not doing, simply being present in each other's vicinity. This page gathers what the science is beginning to show: that stillness is not only an individual experience. It can be a shared one.

The work of Balconi and Angioletti, in particular, has opened a window onto this phenomenon. Their 2023 study on dyadic inter-brain EEG coherence during interoceptive hyperscanning found that when two people focus on their breath together, their brains begin to synchronize in the alpha band — the frequency associated with calm, wakeful rest.1

But the story is broader than alpha synchrony. What follows is a look at the converging evidence that stillness, like breath, can be held in common.

The phenomenon
Stillness as shared experience

The idea that stillness could be shared might seem paradoxical. Stillness, after all, is an inward experience — a turning of attention away from the outer world and toward the quiet that underlies it. How could something so private be held in common?

The hyperscanning literature suggests an answer: when two people enter similar states of quiet awareness, their nervous systems begin to resonate at the same frequency. This is not telepathy. It is the measurable consequence of two biological systems settling into the same resting state — the way two pendulums on the same beam will eventually swing together.

Dyadic inter-brain EEG coherence induced by interoceptive hyperscanning reveals that shared breath attention increases alpha-band synchrony in frontopolar regions — the neural signature of two nervous systems resting in the same rhythm.
— Balconi & Angioletti, Scientific Reports, 2023

The key mechanism appears to be interoceptive attention — the direction of awareness toward internal bodily signals (breath, heartbeat, the felt sense of being). When both members of a dyad are attending inward, their external rhythms converge. And when those rhythms converge, the sense of separateness softens.

The evidence
What the research shows

The evidence for shared stillness draws on several converging lines of research:

  • Inter-brain alpha coherence — Balconi & Angioletti (2023) demonstrated that pairs focusing on breath together show increased EEG coherence in the alpha band (8–12 Hz), particularly in frontopolar regions associated with interoceptive awareness and self-regulation. This effect was stronger during shared breath focus than during a control condition.1
  • Autonomic coupling during shared rest — When two people sit in silence together, their heart rates and respiratory patterns show measurable convergence over time. This coupling occurs without verbal instruction or conscious effort — it appears to be an automatic property of co-regulation.2
  • Broadband neural alignment — Beyond the alpha band, shared stillness has been associated with increased synchrony across delta, theta, and alpha bands simultaneously — a broad-spectrum neural alignment that suggests not just shared rhythm but shared state.3
  • Interoceptive accuracy and dyadic rapport — Individuals with higher interoceptive accuracy (the ability to perceive their own heartbeat) show stronger autonomic coupling during joint stillness tasks, suggesting that the capacity for inward attention facilitates shared rest.4

The mechanism
How shared stillness works

The mechanism of shared stillness appears to operate through what researchers call interpersonal physiological coupling — a process in which the autonomic nervous systems of two people begin to oscillate at the same frequency during periods of co-present rest.

This is distinct from other forms of synchrony. Unlike joint action (walking together, playing music), shared stillness does not involve external coordination. Unlike emotional contagion (catching someone's laughter or distress), it does not depend on shared affect. It is a quieter, subtler alignment — two systems discovering the same resting frequency because they are in each other's presence.

A reflection from the literature: "The convergence of autonomic states during shared rest suggests that the human nervous system is not only a self-regulating system but also an other-regulating one. We do not merely co-exist. We co-regulate — even in stillness, perhaps especially in stillness."

Balconi and Angioletti's work suggests that the frontopolar cortex — a brain region involved in metacognitive awareness and self-reflection — plays a key role in this process. During shared breath focus, frontopolar alpha synchrony increases, creating a neural bridge between the two individuals' interoceptive attention. Each person attending inward, together.

Shared stillness is not two people being quiet in the same room. It is two nervous systems discovering that they rest at the same frequency — and that the discovery itself is part of the rest.

The invitation
Try it with someone

You don't need EEG equipment to test this. You don't need to understand the neuroscience. You just need another person willing to sit in stillness with you for a few minutes.

Sit facing each other, or side by side looking at the same screen. Open the shared breathing circle on two devices, or share one. Breathe with the circles for a few minutes. Don't try to synchronize. Just breathe.

What you might notice — what the research suggests is real — is a quiet sense of alignment. Not dramatic. Not mystical. Just the felt sense of two nervous systems finding the same quiet place.

The science says this is real. The practice says you don't need the science to feel it. But if you're the kind of person who needs to know why before you can try — the science is here, and it is sound.
